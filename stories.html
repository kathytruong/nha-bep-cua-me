The memories behind the meals

A memory of Mẹ — Kim Oanh

"The pot that never went cold"

Mẹ's phở pot lived on the back burner for two days at a time. She'd start it before anyone woke up — bones from the Asian grocery on Stockton, a knob of ginger charred directly on the gas flame, star anise she kept in a tin above the fridge.

The house would smell like broth by the time we got home from school. She never wrote anything down. "Nước trong là được," she'd say — when the broth is clear, it's ready. She'd skim it every hour, patient in a way none of us inherited.

We'd eat it for dinner, and then again for breakfast. The pot just kept going. When someone came over — a neighbor, a cousin passing through — there was always enough. That was the rule. There was always enough.

A memory of Dì Hương & Dì Lan

"The kitchen on Sunday afternoons"

Sundays were when the aunts came over. Dì Hương would arrive first, always with a bag of something — rau muống from the garden, or a container of thịt kho she'd made the night before because "it's better the next day."

Dì Lan would come later, and the kitchen would get loud. They'd talk over each other in Vietnamese while we sat at the table eating, understanding maybe half of it but feeling all of it. Someone was always frying shallots. Someone was always telling someone else their fire was too high.

We didn't know we were supposed to be paying attention. We thought there would be more Sundays. We thought we'd always be able to call and ask, "How much fish sauce?" and hear Mẹ laugh and say, "You'll know."

In loving memory

For Mẹ — Kim Oanh.
